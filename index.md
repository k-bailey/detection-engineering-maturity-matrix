Detection engineering has long been a function of the incident response team. However, over the last several years, it has gained momentum, becoming a dedicated and more well-defined role within many security operations teams. Many great articles and presentations exist (see Medium) on the purpose of detection engineering and how it fits into a broader Security Operations team. This matrix aims to help the community better measure the capabilities and maturity of their detection function and provide a high-level roadmap for organizations looking to either build a team or expand an existing one. (https://kyle-bailey.medium.com/detection-engineering-maturity-matrix-f4f3181a5cc7)

| Category | Subcategory | Defined | Managed | Optimized |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| People | Leadership | - Leadership has basic understanding of detection processes and challenges but limited resources or different priorities may exist. | - Leadership advocates for detection as a dedicated function.<br>- The size of the team and the resources needed may not be fully understood. | - Leadership advocates for involvement in detection processes across the business, as well as necessary tools, licensing, and staff. |
| People | Team | - Ad-hoc team building/managing detection content (i.e., as-needed task owned by incident response or other security individuals).<br>- SME's in none or very few detection domains (i.e. network, host, cloud, etc.) | - Dedicated individuals performing detection work full time (This could be detection & response engineers on a rotation or a dedicated team).<br>- SME’s on some tools & log sources, informally defined domain ownership. | - Dedicated team with defined SME's for all detection domains (host, network, application, cloud, etc.) |
| Process | Detection Process | - Detection strategy, workflow, and process do not exist.<br>- Detection quality depends greatly on the understanding of the individual performing the work.<br>- No backlog or prioritization of known gaps.<br>- Little or no active maintenance or monitoring of existing detection. | - A detection strategy and workflow are defined and followed.<br>- Approval and handoff processes are loosely defined for releasing new detection.<br>- Work is prioritized in an ad-hoc way with little to no input from threat intel or others.<br>- Maintenance and monitoring are performed but are ad-hoc and generally done reactively. | - Detection strategy is defined and continuously iterated on.<br>- Defined review and approval processes exist for new and updated detection, and incident response is heavily involved prior to deployment.<br>- Work is prioritized using input from threat intel, and threat modeling with technology SME's.<br>- Maintenance and monitoring are continuous and most SIEM and detection failures are identified proactively. |
| Process | Metrics | - Little to no detection-related metrics exist.<br>- Metadata and aggregation methods or tools may not exist to collect or report on metric data.<br>- Detection is inconsistently mapped to MITRE ATT&CK or another control framework, but there is no formal tracking | - Some metrics exist for categories such as alert fidelity, mean time to detect, and automated resolutions.<br>- Alert metadata is consistently applied but may have room for improvement or not is not consistently aggregated into actionable data.<br>- Detection metadata includes MITRE ATT&CK TIDs but aggregation of coverage may not occur or is manual. | - KPIs are well-defined and goals are well understood.<br>- Automated methods exist for collecting and reporting on most metrics.<br>- Applicable MITRE ATT&CK TID coverage is automatically tabulated and displayed across nearly all detection sources. |
| Technology | Detection-as-Code | - Detection-as-code principles are not followed or prioritized. | - Detection as code principles are used as a north star but technical components (e.g., testing) may not be fully built out or utilized.<br>- Detection is stored in version control and deployed to production using a CI/CD pipeline.<br>- Linting & testing in CI occur in a very limited way or not at all. | - Detection as code is engrained in the team culture.<br>- Version control, review and approval, linting, and testing are baked into the deployment pipeline.<br>- Some detection logic is continuously tested end-to-end in an automated way. |
| Technology | SIEM | - Log outages and detection errors/failures are not alerted on or well known.<br>- Log ingest delays are not tracked. | - Log source health alerting exists for critical sources.<br>- Most log sources are delivered to the SIEM within minutes but overall log latency may not be well understood. | - The SIEM allows for an expressive language to enable complex detection logic.<br>- Most detection logic is run in real-time.<br>- Robust log health alerting exists for all sources.<br>- Most log sources are delivered to the SIEM within minutes. Those that do not are due to provider limitations. |
| Technology | Visibility | - Visibility across the environment is not well understood.<br>- Visibility is inconsistent and some sources critical for custom detection may be missing.<br>- Tool/SIEM limitations prevent some log sources from being ingested. | - Log sources are cataloged and prioritized.<br>- Most critical log sources are available in the SIEM. | - The detection team defines critical log sources and ensures they are present in the SIEM.<br>- Log providers agree to SLAs on outages and delivery latency. |
| Detection | Detection Content | - Primarily rely on out-of-the-box detection content, little to no customization or custom rules are built.<br>- Detection is primarily IOC focused, few behavioral TTP detections exist | - Detection content is tuned to the environment where applicable.<br>- More behavioral-based detections exist, new detection is mostly TTP-focused (where possible) | - Focused primarily on behavioral/TTP detection logic. ML & statistical-based detection models are applied where applicable.<br>- There is an intense focus on risk-based alerting, leveraging as much lateral (additional events) & external context (enrichment) as possible to estimate the appropriate risk of an alert. |
| Detection | Response Experience | - All detection logic is treated equally in priority<br>- All alerts must be manually reviewed and interacted with by the incident response team or on-call.<br>- Little to no enrichment occurs in alerts<br>- More alerts are generated daily than can be handled by incident response. | - The priority of an alert is clearly displayed for responders.<br>- Alert enrichment occurs from most data sources and is actively being expanded.<br>- Limited workflows that can be used for automated remediation exist but are expanding. | - Alerts are enriched with the relevant context in order to provide incident response an accurate risk picture.<br>- New detection must have automation and enrichment before being released to production. |
| Detection | Threat Operations | - Threats are not simulated for building or testing, historical data is used to build new detection.<br>- Red or purple teaming does not occur outside of mandated pentests. | - Detection creation is prioritized loosely using threat data (known, likely threats)<br>- Purple team exercises are run ad-hoc, potentially loosely driven by threat intel or incidents.<br>- Red team testing occurs on a regular basis. | - Detection creation is prioritized based on known and active threats to the org as identified by threat intel with risk input from other teams (i.e Security engineering, architecture, risk).<br>- Purple team exercises are constantly run to validate and improve detection and response capabilities.<br>- Mature Red team capabilities work closely with detection and response daily. |
