| Category | Subcategory | Defined | Managed | Optimized |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| People | Leadership | <ul><li>Leadership has basic understanding of detection processes and challenges but limited resources or different priorities may exist.</li></ul> | <ul><li>Leadership advocates for detection as a dedicated function.</li><li>The size of the team and the resources needed may not be fully understood.</li></ul> | <ul><li>Leadership advocates for involvement in detection processes across the business, as well as necessary tools, licensing, and staff.</li></ul> |
| People | Team | <ul><li>Ad-hoc team building/managing detection content (i.e., as-needed task owned by incident response or other security individuals).</li><li>SME's in none or very few detection domains (i.e. network, host, cloud, etc.)</li></ul> | <ul><li>Dedicated individuals performing detection work full time (This could be detection & response engineers on a rotation or a dedicated team).</li><li>SME’s on some tools & log sources, informally defined domain ownership.</li></ul> | <ul><li>Dedicated team with defined SME's for all detection domains (host, network, application, cloud, etc.)</li></ul> |
| Process | Detection Process | <ul><li>Detection strategy, workflow, and process do not exist.</li><li>Detection quality depends greatly on the understanding of the individual performing the work.</li><li>No backlog or prioritization of known gaps.</li><li>Little or no active maintenance or monitoring of existing detection.</li></ul> | <ul><li>A detection strategy and workflow are defined and followed.</li><li>Approval and handoff processes are loosely defined for releasing new detection.</li><li>Work is prioritized in an ad-hoc way with little to no input from threat intel or others.</li><li>Maintenance and monitoring are performed but are ad-hoc and generally done reactively.</li></ul> | <ul><li>Detection strategy is defined and continuously iterated on.</li><li>Defined review and approval processes exist for new and updated detection, and incident response is heavily involved prior to deployment.</li><li>Work is prioritized using input from threat intel, and threat modeling with technology SME's.</li><li>Maintenance and monitoring are continuous and most SIEM and detection failures are identified proactively.</li></ul> |
| Process | Metrics | <ul><li>Little to no detection-related metrics exist.</li><li>Metadata and aggregation methods or tools may not exist to collect or report on metric data.</li><li>Detection is inconsistently mapped to MITRE ATT&CK or another control framework, but there is no formal tracking</li></ul> | <ul><li>Some metrics exist for categories such as alert fidelity, mean time to detect, and automated resolutions.</li><li>Alert metadata is consistently applied but may have room for improvement or not is not consistently aggregated into actionable data.</li><li>Detection metadata includes MITRE ATT&CK TIDs but aggregation of coverage may not occur or is manual.</li></ul> | <ul><li>KPIs are well-defined and goals are well understood.</li><li>Automated methods exist for collecting and reporting on most metrics.</li><li>Applicable MITRE ATT&CK TID coverage is automatically tabulated and displayed across nearly all detection sources.</li></ul> |
| Technology | Detection-as-Code | <ul><li>Detection-as-code principles are not followed or prioritized.</li></ul> | <ul><li>Detection as code principles are used as a north star but technical components (e.g., testing) may not be fully built out or utilized.</li><li>Detection is stored in version control and deployed to production using a CI/CD pipeline.</li><li>Linting & testing in CI occur in a very limited way or not at all.</li></ul> | <ul><li>Detection as code is engrained in the team culture.</li><li>Version control, review and approval, linting, and testing are baked into the deployment pipeline.</li><li>Some detection logic is continuously tested end-to-end in an automated way.</li></ul> |
| Technology | SIEM | <ul><li>Log outages and detection errors/failures are not alerted on or well known.</li><li>Log ingest delays are not tracked.</li></ul> | <ul><li>Log source health alerting exists for critical sources.</li><li>Most log sources are delivered to the SIEM within minutes but overall log latency may not be well understood.</li></ul> | <ul><li>The SIEM allows for an expressive language to enable complex detection logic.</li><li>Most detection logic is run in real-time.</li><li>Robust log health alerting exists for all sources.</li><li>Most log sources are delivered to the SIEM within minutes. Those that do not are due to provider limitations.</li></ul> |
| Technology | Visibility | <ul><li>Visibility across the environment is not well understood.</li><li>Visibility is inconsistent and some sources critical for custom detection may be missing.</li><li>Tool/SIEM limitations prevent some log sources from being ingested.</li></ul> | <ul><li>Log sources are cataloged and prioritized.</li><li>Most critical log sources are available in the SIEM.</li></ul> | <ul><li>The detection team defines critical log sources and ensures they are present in the SIEM.</li><li>Log providers agree to SLAs on outages and delivery latency.</li></ul> |
| Detection | Detection Content | <ul><li>Primarily rely on out-of-the-box detection content, little to no customization or custom rules are built.</li><li>Detection is primarily IOC focused, few behavioral TTP detections exist</li></ul> | <ul><li>Detection content is tuned to the environment where applicable.</li><li>More behavioral-based detections exist, new detection is mostly TTP-focused (where possible)</li></ul> | <ul><li>Focused primarily on behavioral/TTP detection logic. ML & statistical-based detection models are applied where applicable.</li><li>There is an intense focus on risk-based alerting, leveraging as much lateral (additional events) & external context (enrichment) as possible to estimate the appropriate risk of an alert.</li></ul> |
| Detection | Response Experience | <ul><li>All detection logic is treated equally in priority</li><li>All alerts must be manually reviewed and interacted with by the incident response team or on-call.</li><li>Little to no enrichment occurs in alerts</li><li>More alerts are generated daily than can be handled by incident response.</li></ul> | <ul><li>The priority of an alert is clearly displayed for responders.</li><li>Alert enrichment occurs from most data sources and is actively being expanded.</li><li>Limited workflows that can be used for automated remediation exist but are expanding.</li></ul> | <ul><li>Alerts are enriched with the relevant context in order to provide incident response an accurate risk picture.</li><li>New detection must have automation and enrichment before being released to production.</li></ul> |
| Detection | Threat Operations | <ul><li>Threats are not simulated for building or testing, historical data is used to build new detection.</li><li>Red or purple teaming does not occur outside of mandated pentests.</li></ul> | <ul><li>Detection creation is prioritized loosely using threat data (known, likely threats)</li><li>Purple team exercises are run ad-hoc, potentially loosely driven by threat intel or incidents.</li><li>Red team testing occurs on a regular basis.</li></ul> | <ul><li>Detection creation is prioritized based on known and active threats to the org as identified by threat intel with risk input from other teams (i.e Security engineering, architecture, risk).</li><li>Purple team exercises are constantly run to validate and improve detection and response capabilities.</li><li>Mature Red team capabilities work closely with detection and response daily.</li></ul> |
